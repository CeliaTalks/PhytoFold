Making agriculture more environmentally friendly through Integrated Pest Management.

What Is IPM?

Integrated Pest Management

A shift in agriculture from conventional chemical pesticides to more sustainable and eco-friendly approaches to controlling pests, weeds, and diseases. IPM aims to manage pest damage in the most economical way that minimises harm to humans, other organisms, and the environment.

The IPM approach is not confined solely to agriculture — it has relevant applications to gardening and land management more broadly.

Framework

The Four Pillars of IPM

Aim

To recognise quickly when there is a pest problem, so that control measures can be used only when necessary, rather than pre-emptively.

Methods

  • Inspection of crops
  • Pest, weed, and disease identification
  • Forecasting and assessing levels of pest populations and diseases

Aim

To ensure that control measures are only used when necessary, thereby limiting excessive use of measures.

Methods

  • A threshold for crop damage is set, above which further pest damage is economically important.
  • If this threshold is surpassed, then control action is taken.

Aim

To prevent pests from becoming a significant problem.

Methods — Cultural

Focused on the crops grown and the methods used to grow them

  • Crop rotation
  • Cultivation and tillage practices
  • Growing pest and disease-resistant varieties
  • Using trap crops to draw away pests
  • Hygiene measures — regular cleansing of machinery and equipment

Early Planting — Late July to Early August

Allows time for crop establishment and development before the peak activity of adult beetles, such that WOSR crops are more resilient against feeding damage.

Late Planting — September Onwards

Allows WOSR to avoid the peak migration of adult CSFB, reducing adult feeding pressure during crop establishment. However, late season conditions are likely to be colder and wetter, which are unfavourable for WOSR crop establishment.

Aim

Effective, eco-friendly methods to target pest populations when preventative actions are no longer effective. To use pest-specific methods that minimise off-target effects on other organisms such as pollinators and the environment.

Methods

Biological

  • Introduce natural predators
  • Using natural parasites or pathogens to control pest populations

Physical / Mechanical

  • Traps, barriers, mowing or hand-pulling to physically remove pests

Cultural

  • Modifying the environment to make it less favourable for pests (soil management, strategic planting times)

Chemical Pesticides

  • Using the minimal effective dose and application frequency
  • Targeted application using precision technology such as spot treatments
  • Biopesticides

Biopesticide — Definition

Pesticides derived from natural materials that control pests through natural processes.

Key Features

  • Highly specific with limited off-target effects on pollinators and humans
  • Biodegradable, which reduces persistence and environmental impacts

More detail on biopesticide strategies — including how they are being developed and applied to CSFB — is covered further down this page.

Why It Matters

Benefits of IPM

  • Reduced chemical usage and more targeted application reduces chemical runoff
  • Specific biopesticides have limited off-target effects
  • Biopesticides are biodegradable, which reduces long-term environmental impacts from agriculture
  • Diversifies the methods used to combat pests, reducing the selection pressure that drives resistance development in pest populations
  • Uses sustainable and preventative care to reduce the long-term costs associated with pest damage and repeated chemical treatments
  • Reduces broad-spectrum pesticide use, protecting pollinators and other beneficial insects
  • Promotes ecological balance by preserving natural pest predators and supporting wider biodiversity in agricultural landscapes
Biopesticides

Biopesticide Strategies

Biopesticides represent one of the most promising frontiers in sustainable pest management. Unlike broad-spectrum chemical pesticides, biopesticide strategies are designed to target specific pest species with minimal impact on surrounding ecosystems.

Microbial Biopesticides

Naturally occurring microorganisms — including bacteria, fungi, viruses, and nematodes — that infect and kill target pest species. These exploit existing biological relationships between pathogens and their hosts.

Biochemical Biopesticides

Naturally derived substances that disrupt pest behaviour or physiology — such as insect pheromones that interfere with mating, or plant extracts that deter feeding — without direct toxicity.

Protein-Based Biopesticides

Proteins engineered or derived from natural sources that target insect-specific biological pathways. The development of insect-specific protein biopesticides is a rapidly progressing field, driven by the incorporation of AI protein design technologies into pest science.

Because biopesticides act through highly specific mechanisms, they are well suited to integration within an IPM framework — effective at controlling target pests while preserving the beneficial insects, soil organisms, and wider biodiversity that conventional pesticides harm.
