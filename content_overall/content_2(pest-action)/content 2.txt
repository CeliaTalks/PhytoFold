The interaction between CSFB and WOSR CSFB March Đ May * Larvae exit host plant & pupate in soil (8-12 weeks)June Đ August * Adults emerge & feed on mature OSR cropso Brief period of intense feeding to accumulate energy reserves (that can be catabolised during diapause to maintain energy homeostasis throughout)* Adults migrate to sheltered, moist places before entering aestivation o 25-day period of summer diapause (like hibernation)o More detail É * Why: to reduce the desiccation risk during summer* When: in anticipation of unfavourable conditions, such as high temperatures * How: CSFB are univoltine insects so aestivation is obligate (essential) and genetically predetermined * What: During which, reduction in metabolic rate (decreased VCO2 and NADH dehydrogenase abundance) and behavioural activity September Đ November * Adults emerge from aestivation and migrate up to 2-3 miles (equivalent to 3-4 km) to young WOSR seedlings to feed o Peak migration in mid-September when air temperature is above 16C* How do CSFB locate WOSR: o Olfactory detection * OSR crops release VOCs such as ITCS (isothiocyanates) and GLSs (glucosinolates) molecules in response to cell damage, which are detected by CSFB and help guide more CSFB to OSR * Sexual dimorphism in behavioural response, with females showing enhanced sensitivity to ITCs * Type of glucosinolate (GSL), required by CSFB o Visual cues o Chance encounters o Detection and behavioural response to VOCs ** colonising population typically contains more males than females (60:40), which may result from their higher activity levels due to mate finding activities, or their earlier emergence * Adult flight muscles lost through atrophy Females lay eggs near base of WOSR in the soil (over a 7-month period)December Đ February * Eggs hatch after about 37 days and penetrate host plant to feed o Time taken to hatch is temperature dependent (37 days at 10C and 70 days at 6C)OSR March -
