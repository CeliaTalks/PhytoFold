Foundations

Protein Basics

Protein structure exists across several scales — from the linear primary sequence through to the quaternary assembly. Click each level to explore further.

The primary structure is the linear sequence of amino acids held together by peptide bonds.

It underpins all protein structures and interactions — as it determines how the protein will fold, the primary structure has cascading effects on every higher level of organisation.

The primary sequence folds and forms hydrogen bonds that dictate the arrangement of the polypeptide backbone, with van der Waals and hydrophobic interactions supporting stability.

Hydrogen Bonding Occurs between the amino and carboxyl groups of the backbone
Van der Waals Weak forces of attraction between closely packed atoms
Hydrophobic Interactions Hydrophobic side chains orient inward to avoid water; hydrophilic side chains act conversely

Main structural motifs:

  • Alpha helix
  • Beta-pleated sheet
  • Turns and loops

The secondary structure folds further to form the final 3D structure that determines the protein's function. This involves hydrogen bonds, van der Waals and hydrophobic interactions, as well as ionic bonds and disulfide bonds.

Ionic Bonds Attraction between oppositely charged amino acid side chains
Disulfide Bonds Covalent links between sulphur atoms of two cysteine amino acids

The structure formed by the interaction of two or more polypeptide chains in their tertiary structure, that together act as one functional unit.

Fundamentals

Protein Logic Underpinning AI Technologies

Protein-protein interactions (PPIs) are determined by the complexity of structural protein features and are essential to all biological processes, such as enzymatic chemical reactions. The strength of a PPI can be scored with binding affinity — which refers to how well a binder (substrate) binds to a receptor.

If the primary protein structure is modified it has cascading effects — from altering protein folding to the final tertiary structure and how it interacts. This is the concept that underpins all protein design technologies.

AI protein design can generate potential biopesticide candidates much faster than traditional methods of crystallography, by altering protein properties and thereby their interactions.

Stage 1

Backpropagation from Binding Site Shape

Molecules are backpropagated from the shape of the binding site rather than from the primary amino acid sequence. This allows BindCraft to generate binder structures that are geometrically complementary to the target receptor before any sequence is assigned.

Stage 2

Sequence Redesign via ProteinMPNN

At each iteration the binder sequence is redesigned using an AF2 monomer model (ProteinMPNN). This stage focuses on residues not interacting with the target, making the designed protein more realistic and realisable in synthesis.

Properties optimised at this stage:

  • Interface and binder pLDDT
  • Interface PTM loss
  • Inter and intra-molecular pAE (amount of contacts)
  • Radius of gyration loss for globular proteins
  • Negative helicity loss (alpha helices are prioritised)
Stage 3

Energy Cost of Unsaturated Hydrogen Bonds

Considers the energy cost of unsaturated hydrogen bonds that are overlooked by AF2. This additional refinement step improves the physical plausibility of the designed binder and increases the likelihood of successful experimental validation.
