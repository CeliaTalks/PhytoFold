CSFB Pest Action

The Cabbage Stem Flea Beetle's annual life cycle is synchronised with oilseed rape, such that it attacks when oilseed rape is most vulnerable.

Overview

The Interaction Between CSFB and WOSR

The lifecycle of Psylliodes chrysocephala (CSFB) and Brassica napus (WOSR) are intimately synchronised. CSFB times its adult emergence and migration to coincide precisely with the period when WOSR is most biologically vulnerable — the seedling establishment phase.

Critical overlap: Peak CSFB adult migration (September–November) coincides exactly with OSR seedling establishment — the crop's most vulnerable window, before it reaches the protective 4–5 leaf stage.

Annual Lifecycle

CSFB Annual Life Cycle

March – May

Pupation

Larvae exit the host plant and pupate in the soil for 8–12 weeks, developing into adult beetles ready to emerge in early summer.

June – August

Feeding & Summer Diapause

Adults emerge and feed briefly but intensively on mature OSR crops, accumulating energy reserves. They then enter aestivation — a 25-day period of summer diapause.

WhyTo reduce desiccation risk during summer
WhenIn anticipation of unfavourable conditions (high temperatures)
HowCSFB are univoltine insects — aestivation is obligate and genetically predetermined
WhatReduction in metabolic rate (↓ VCO₂ and NADH dehydrogenase abundance) and behavioural activity
September – November

Migration & Egg-Laying

Adults migrate up to 2–3 miles (3–4 km) to young WOSR seedlings. Peak migration occurs in mid-September when air temperature exceeds 16°C.

How CSFB locate WOSR:

  • Olfactory: WOSR releases ITCs and GLSs in response to cell damage. Females show enhanced sensitivity to ITCs.
  • Visual cues and chance encounters

Colonising population is typically ~60% male. Females lay eggs near the WOSR base over a 7-month period.

December – February

Egg Hatching & Host Entry

Eggs hatch after approximately 37 days and larvae penetrate the host plant. Hatching time is temperature-dependent:

37 days
at 10°C
70 days
at 6°C
Host Crop

OSR Annual Life Cycle

March – May

Flowering & Pod Formation

The crop enters rapid growth phase. Yellow flowers bloom across fields, followed by pod formation as pollination occurs.

June – August

Harvest

The mature crop is harvested. Pods are collected and processed to extract rapeseed oil and produce protein-rich meal for livestock feed.

September – November ⚠

Seedling Establishment

Most vulnerable period. This window coincides exactly with peak CSFB adult migration, exposing young seedlings to maximum pest pressure before they reach the protective 4–5 leaf stage.

Freshly sown seed germinates and seedlings emerge. Adult CSFB feeding at this stage can stunt growth severely or kill plants outright.

December – February

Stem Elongation

Plants grow through winter as stems elongate. However, CSFB larvae that hatched in autumn continue their internal feeding throughout this period, mining stems and weakening the plant's structure.

Mechanism of Damage

How CSFB Damage WOSR

CSFB pose a two-level problem against WOSR, with adults and larvae inducing distinct and compounding effects at different times of year.

Adult Damage

Surface feeding on young leaves

WhenLate August to mid-September — peak damage coincides with peak adult migration
HowAdults feed on young leaves and cotyledons, creating characteristic "shot-holing" patterns
ImpactStunts WOSR growth and slows crop establishment. Most severe before the 4–5 leaf stage. Can destroy growing plants entirely.

Larval Damage

Internal feeding inside stems

WhenLate autumn through to spring, continuing all winter
HowLarvae burrow into WOSR plants, mining petioles and side stems until reaching the main stem — internal feeding entirely hidden from view
ImpactWeakens the main stem, can collapse plants. Internal damage increases susceptibility to light leaf spot, phoma, and botrytis.
The Challenge

Why CSFB Are So Difficult to Control

Several biological traits make CSFB uniquely challenging to manage — especially without a systemic seed treatment, where larvae burrow out of reach of all contact-acting foliar insecticides.

Adults can live up to 10 months, providing a prolonged window to cause damage and lay eggs — meaning a single cohort of beetles can inflict damage across multiple seasons.

Each female can lay 200+ eggs over a 7-month period, rapidly building population numbers even from a small initial infestation.

Larvae burrow inside stems, completely out of reach of contact-acting foliar insecticides. Once inside the plant, no currently available pesticide can reach them.

CSFB are only accessible to pesticides during the brief adult feeding phase before they enter the plant. Miss this window and chemical control becomes ineffective.

CSFB can survive on related Brassica weeds between crops, maintaining populations even without OSR present — making population suppression through crop rotation ineffective alone.

CSFB adapts quickly through two distinct resistance mechanisms: knockdown resistance mutations in the sodium channel target site, and metabolic resistance via enhanced detoxification enzymes — a combination that renders pyrethroids effectively useless.

Without a systemic seed treatment (banned since 2013), larvae inside stems are completely unreachable by any currently approved pesticide. This is arguably the single biggest factor behind the current management crisis.

Adults move quickly from plant to plant and can fly several kilometres, readily escaping natural enemies and rapidly re-colonising treated fields — making both chemical and biological control strategies difficult to sustain.
