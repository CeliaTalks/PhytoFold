The Problem

Oilseed Rape challenged by Cabbage Stem Flea Beetle

Psylliodes chrysocephala, the Cabbage Stem Flea Beetle (CSFB), has become the greatest biological threat to Britain's most important oil crop, and every existing chemical defence is failing.

Background

Threat to Oilseed Rape

The Cabbage Stem Flea Beetle (Psylliodes chrysocephala, CSFB) has become one of the biggest threats to winter oilseed rape (Brassica napus, WOSR), the crop behind those bright yellow fields you see each spring. CSFB may seem a peculiar name, but WOSR in fact belongs to the cabbage (Brassica) family.

WOSR is one of the most widely grown crops across Northern Europe, known for its bright yellow flowers and for producing rapeseed oil, the UK's most popular cooking oil, accounting for 40% of total edible oil in the UK. Rapeseed oil is a staple in many foods, from mayonnaise to crisps to bread, and a vital component of livestock feed.

Food System Dependency

Rapeseed Oil Is in Everything

OSR is the only commercially viable oil crop in the UK, accounting for 40% of total edible oil, making it irreplaceable in the food system. Its decline doesn't just affect cooking oil; the consequences extend across the entire food supply chain.

From fried foods and condiments to commercial baked goods, ready meals, and livestock feed, the uses of rapeseed oil touch almost every aspect of the modern diet. Losing this crop is a direct threat to food prices and food security for everyone.

OSR is a staple in mayonnaise, crisps, bread, and livestock feed, with no domestically viable alternative oil crop to replace it.

Global Spread

A Threat Across Northern Europe & North America

Consequently, CSFB are found all across northern Europe and North America. Warming temperatures have increased the potential range of CSFB, pushing populations into previously unsuitable climates.

The spread of CSFB is not a localised problem — it represents a pan-European and transatlantic agricultural challenge, threatening some of the world's most productive oilseed rape regions.

The Crisis

Failing Management Strategies

CSFB were previously managed using neonicotinoid insecticides; however, the European Union banned this class of insecticide in 2013 due to their off-target effects on important pollinators. Since then, farmers turned to pyrethroid insecticides, but resistance is now widespread.

Insecticide

Compounds used to manage and deter insect pest populations that affect food production.

Neonicotinoid — Why Banned

Negative effects on wild bee populations, specifically reducing the growth and reproduction of colonies (Rundlöf et al., 2015). Their influence also extends to birds — those consuming neonicotinoids directly through treated seed or indirectly via insects show reduced migration ability (Eng et al., 2017) and significant weight loss (Hallmann et al., 2014). These pesticides are particularly problematic due to high persistence in soils and leaching into water systems, spreading environmental harm far beyond fields.

Why Neonicotinoids Were Banned (2013)

  • Reduced wild bee colony growth and reproduction
  • Impaired bird migration ability and caused significant weight loss in exposed birds
  • High persistence in soils with leaching into water systems
  • Off-target effects spreading environmental harm far beyond treated fields

56% Mean Pyrethroid Resistance

Mean resistance of 56% across UK populations, with some at 100%. Two-fold resistance — knockdown mutations and metabolic resistance — renders pyrethroids effectively useless.

There is currently no effective management strategy against CSFB — resulting in higher and higher beetle populations across the UK.

Impact on WOSR

Collapsing Crop Yields

Without an effective management strategy, WOSR yields have substantially reduced due to damage from CSFB. Average UK yields have decreased by 0.63 t/ha since 2014.

To avoid the costs of failed WOSR crops, many farms have abandoned the crop entirely — with a reduction in UK land growing WOSR by 35% between 2012 and 2019.

Pollinator Crisis

OSR flowers in early spring when few other crops do, providing a critical food source for bees. With 35% less OSR, pollinators lose this vital window — triggering cascading effects from reduced pollination to decreased biodiversity.

Agricultural Sustainability

OSR breaks pest and disease cycles in cereal rotations, improves soil structure, and boosts wheat yields. Without it, farming becomes less sustainable — more fertilisers, less diverse cropping, declining yields.
