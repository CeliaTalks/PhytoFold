Protein basics Protein structure exists across several scales from primary to tertiary and quaternary structure. * Primary structure o Linear sequence of amino acids that are held together by peptide bonds o underpins all protein structures and interactions, as it determines how the protein will fold * secondary structure o the primary sequence folds and forms hydrogen bonds that dictates the arrangement of the polypeptide backbone with van der Waals and hydrophobic interactions supporting stability and foldingo hydrogen bonding * occurs between the amino and carboxyl groups of the backbone o van der waals * weak forces of attraction between closely packed atoms o hydrophobic interactions * hydrophobic amino acid side chains orient themselves within the interior of the protein structure to avoid water and hydrophilic amino acids act conversely o main structures* alpha helix * beta-pleated sheet * turns and loops * tertiary structure o the secondary structure folds further to form the final 3D structure that determines the proteinŐs function. This involves hydrogen bonds, van der waals and hydrophobic interactions as well as ionic bonds, disulfide bonds, and hydrophobic interactionso ionic bonds * attraction between oppositely charged amino acid side chains o disulfide bonds * covalent links between sulphur atoms of two cysteine amino acids * quaternary structure o the structure formed by the interaction of two or more polypeptide chains in their tertiary structure, that acts as one functional unit fundamentals that underpin all protein design technology Protein-protein interactions (PPIs) are determined by the complexity of structural protein features and are essential to all biological processes, such as enzymatic chemical reactions. The strength of PPI can be scored/valued with binding affinity which refers to how well a binder (substrate) binds to a receptor É Hence, if the primary protein structure is modified it has cascading effects from altering protein folding to the final tertiary structure and how it interacts. This is the concept that underpins protein design technologies.AI protein design can generate potential biopesticide candidates much faster than traditional methods of crystallography, by altering protein properties and thereby their interactions. Different technologies // basic overview of how they work Rosetta * old school physics-based diffusion model that relies on energy calculations to produce structures with the lowest energy state. * Based upon dG/dSASAAlpha Fold 2 (AF2)* Function: predicts the tertiary structure of a protein (how it folds) from the primary amino acid sequence* allows sequences to be validated before experimentation and is much quicker Bind Craft Đ what we are using * function: uses AF2 to designs binder proteins that will bind to the binding site of a selected/specified receptor how it works * stage 1* Backpropagation molecules from the shape of the binding site instead of the primary amino acid sequence * Stage 32* At each iteration the binder sequence is redesigned using an AF2 monomer model (protein MPNN). This stage focuses on the residues (protein parts) not interacting with the target to make the designed protein more realistic and possible to synthesise. * Properties that are improved * Interface and binder pLDDT* Interface PTM loss* Inter and intra-molecular pAE (amount of contacts)* Radius of gyration loss for globular proteins * Negative helicity loss (alpha helices are prioritised -good)* Stage 3* Considers energy cost of unsaturated Hydrogen bonds that are overlooked by AF2Pros and cons (as a table)* Advantages * Average success rate is 46.3% (higher than physical alternatives)* Facilitates the experimental screening of fewer binders which is more cost effective and faster * Robustness is prioritised in each step * Disadvantages * Backpropagation through AF2 is GPU-intensive which is expensive * The filtering criteria used might exclude potentially good binders * Insensitive to point mutations that affect PPI interfaces
